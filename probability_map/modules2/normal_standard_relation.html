Standardization

$$ Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma} $$

Transform any Normal X to Standard Z

Source: $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$
Changing $\mu, \sigma$ shifts/scales this curve.
Target: $Z \sim N(0, 1)$ Fixed
Always centered at 0, spread 1.
$$ Z = \frac{X - \text{mean}}{\text{sd}} = \frac{X - 2}{1.5} $$

üìò Why Standardize?

By subtracting the mean ($\mu$) and dividing by the standard deviation ($\sigma$), we remove the units of measurement. This allows us to compare different datasets (e.g., comparing height in cm vs weight in kg) on a common "Z-score" scale.

Inverse Transformation

$$ X = \mu + Z \cdot \sigma $$

Recover Normal X from Standard Z

Source: $Z \sim N(0, 1)$ Fixed Base
Standard deviation units (Z-scores).
Result: $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$
$E[X] = \mu, \quad \text{Var}[X] = \sigma^2$
$$ X = \text{mean} + Z \cdot \text{sd} = 2 + Z \cdot 1.5 $$

üìò Practical Application

This direction is often used in simulation. If you want to simulate random numbers for IQ scores ($\mu=100, \sigma=15$), computer software first generates a standard normal number $Z$, then applies $100 + Z \cdot 15$ to get the IQ score.
