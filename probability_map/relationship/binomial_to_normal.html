üìò Central Limit Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) states that the sum of many independent random variables tends toward a normal distribution, regardless of the original distribution.

For $\text{Binomial}(n, p)$, which is the sum of $n$ independent $\text{Bernoulli}(p)$ trials, the CLT implies that as $n$ becomes large, the distribution approaches: $$\text{Binomial}(n,p) \xrightarrow{d} \text{Normal}(\mu = np, \sigma^2 = np(1-p))$$

Standardization: The standardized variable converges to standard normal: $$Z = \frac{X - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

Rule of thumb: The normal approximation is good when $np \geq 10$ and $n(1-p) \geq 10$.

Continuity correction: For better accuracy, use $P(X \leq k) \approx \Phi\left(\dfrac{k + 0.5 - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}\right)$
