üìò Poisson Limit Theorem

When the number of trials $n$ is large and the success probability $p$ is small, with the product $np = \lambda$ held constant, the $\text{Binomial}(n, p)$ distribution converges to $\text{Poisson}(\lambda)$.

Mathematical statement: $$\lim_{n \to \infty} \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!} \quad \text{where } p = \frac{\lambda}{n}$$

Intuition: Consider counting rare events in many trials ‚Äî e.g., typos per page, radioactive decays, or insurance claims.

Key difference in variance:
‚Ä¢ Binomial: $\text{Var}[X] = np(1-p) = \lambda(1 - \lambda/n)$
‚Ä¢ Poisson: $\text{Var}[Y] = \lambda$
As $n \to \infty$: $\lambda(1 - \lambda/n) \to \lambda$
